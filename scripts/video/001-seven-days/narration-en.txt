Sunday, Monday, Tuesday, Wednesday, Thursday, Friday, Saturday. You say these every day. But WHY are they in this order? It's not random. It's a 4,000-year-old astronomical code — and India cracked it.

Ancient astronomers could see 7 moving objects in the sky — the Sun, Moon, and 5 visible planets. They ranked them by orbital speed, from slowest to fastest: Saturn at 29 years. Jupiter at 12. Mars at 2. The Sun at 1 year. Venus at 225 days. Mercury at 88 days. And the Moon at just 27 days.

Here's where it gets brilliant. They assigned each HOUR of the day to one of these 7 planets, cycling through them in orbital speed order. Hour 1 of Saturday — Saturn. Hour 2 — Jupiter. Hour 3 — Mars. And so on, cycling through all 7, three full times through 24 hours, with 3 left over.

Now the key insight: the planet that rules the FIRST hour of the day gives that day its name. Saturday starts with Saturn. Count 24 hours through the sequence — hour 25 — the first hour of the NEXT day — lands on the Sun. That's Sunday.

Count another 24 — you land on the Moon. Monday. Then Mars — Tuesday. Mercury — Wednesday. Jupiter — Thursday. Venus — Friday. And back to Saturn — Saturday.

There's an elegant shortcut. In the orbital speed list, skip every 2 planets: Saturn — skip Jupiter, Mars — land on Sun. Skip Venus, Mercury — land on Moon. The weekday order is the orbital speed order with every 3rd planet selected.

This system spread everywhere — and the proof is in the languages. In Sanskrit: Ravivāsaraḥ — Ravi means Sun. Somavāsaraḥ — Soma means Moon. In French: Lundi — from Luna. Mardi — from Mars. In Japanese: Nichiyōbi — Nichi means Sun. Every culture, same planets, same order.

This 4,000-year-old system isn't just history — it's still alive. Right now, this hour is ruled by a specific planet. That planet influences which activities are auspicious.
