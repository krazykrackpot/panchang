# Why Is a Mahayuga Exactly 4,320,000 Years?

The Surya Siddhanta — a Sanskrit astronomical text from roughly the 5th century CE — defines a Mahayuga as 4,320,000 solar years. That number looks arbitrary until you start dividing it by planetary orbits. Then it stops looking arbitrary and starts looking engineered.

## Every orbit comes out clean

Divide 4,320,000 by Saturn's orbital period in years. You get 146,568. Divide by Jupiter's: 364,220. The Moon: 57,753,336. Mercury: 17,937,060.

Not one remainder. Not one decimal. All nine bodies tracked by the Surya Siddhanta — the Sun, Moon, five planets, the Moon's apogee, and the lunar node (Rahu) — complete whole-number orbits in exactly 4,320,000 years.

These aren't approximations shoved into a round number. 146,568 orbits of Saturn at 29.474 years each gives 4,320,000.00. Not 4,319,998. Not 4,320,003. Exact.

## 4,320,000 is the smallest number that works

I assumed at first that 4,320,000 was chosen for cosmological reasons and the astronomy was fitted into it. So I computed the mathematical minimum — the Least Common Multiple of all nine orbital periods.

The LCM of just the seven planets (Sun through Saturn) is 1,080,000 — only a quarter of the Mahayuga. The cosmological number looked unnecessarily large.

Then I added the Moon's apogee. Its Bhagana number is 488,203.

488,203 is prime.

A prime number has no factors in common with anything. It can't be reduced. The LCM of any set of numbers that includes a prime must be a multiple of that prime. And 488,203 doesn't divide evenly into 1,080,000, or 2,160,000, or 3,240,000. The first multiple of the seven-planet LCM that also accommodates 488,203 complete apogee cycles is... 4,320,000.

The Mahayuga isn't four times larger than it needs to be. It IS the minimum. One prime number — the Moon's apogee — is what forces the entire cosmic cycle to be exactly 4,320,000 years. Not 4 million. Not 4.5 million. 4,320,000 and nothing less.

## The cosmological framework gives the same number

The Surya Siddhanta also derives 4,320,000 from its cosmology. One divine year equals 360 solar years. A Mahayuga spans 12,000 divine years:

12,000 × 360 = 4,320,000

Those 12,000 divine years split across four ages in a 4:3:2:1 ratio — Krita gets 1,728,000 years, Treta 1,296,000, Dvapara 864,000, Kali 432,000. The Sanskrit verse is clean: "The tenth part of this Yuga, multiplied successively by four, three, two and one, gives the length of Krita and the other ages" (Surya Siddhanta 1.17).

Two completely different derivations — astronomical and cosmological — land on the same number. One is number theory (LCM forced by a prime). The other is a philosophical age system (divine years × 360). They agree exactly.

## You can't build this container without knowing what goes inside

To design a time span where nine different orbital periods all produce integer revolution counts, you need to know all nine periods first. The LCM is computed FROM the inputs, not the other way around.

Which means the authors of the Surya Siddhanta possessed all nine orbital periods — including Saturn's 29.474-year orbit (NASA: 29.457, off by 6 days across three decades) and Mercury's 87.970-day orbit (NASA: 87.969, off by 86 seconds) — before they chose the Mahayuga number. The grand cosmic cycle is the last step of the astronomy, not the first.

## How did they get the orbital periods?

The text's own account: revealed by Surya (the Sun) to the Asura Maya at the end of Satya Yuga. Not empirical discovery. Received knowledge.

The observational account: Saturn is the bright yellowish dot that doesn't twinkle, moving one constellation per 2.5 years. Track it at each opposition against the nakshatras — 27 star clusters that grid the sky into 13.3-degree segments. One lifetime gives you two or three complete orbits and a rough period. Five generations of teacher-to-student records give ten orbits and the precision to say 10,765.77 days.

Mercury is the case that doesn't sit comfortably in either account. Never more than 28 degrees from the Sun, visible only at twilight for minutes at a time. The Surya Siddhanta's value is 86 seconds off NASA's. That's hard to explain with naked-eye twilight observation alone.

The Moon's apogee is even stranger. The apogee — the point where the Moon is farthest from Earth — isn't visible at all. You infer it from the Moon's varying apparent speed, which requires tracking its daily motion with enough precision to detect the difference between 12 and 14 degrees per day. 488,203 revolutions in 4,320,000 years gives a period of 3,232 days (8.85 years). The modern value is 3,233 days. Whoever measured this was tracking an invisible geometric point to within a day over nearly nine years.

The two accounts — revelation and observation — don't have to fight. Centuries of observation produce the raw periods. A contemplative or mathematical insight recognises the unifying structure. Spotting that a set of nine orbital periods shares a common cycle isn't a telescopic skill. It's a number-theoretic one. And the claim that a single prime number locks the entire system at exactly 4,320,000 — that's verifiable. Anyone can check it.

## What survived and what didn't

The observational records — palm-leaf logs, observatory notes, generational data — are gone. What survived is nine integers and a divisor. The Bhagana numbers fit in a few verses. They could be chanted, memorised, and transmitted without corruption across fifty generations.

The raw data couldn't survive that. The compressed form could. Same story in every ancient tradition — Ptolemy's model survives but not the Babylonian observation logs it drew on.

## Still computing festival dates

The Bhagana numbers aren't historical curiosities. When a pandit checks tomorrow's tithi, the computation runs on 57,753,336 lunar revolutions per 4,320,000 years. When your family checks whether Thursday is good for a housewarming, they're querying Jupiter's Bhagana of 364,220. Diwali's date, your wedding muhurta, the next eclipse — all trace back to these nine integers.

Babylonian astronomy was equally precise. Greek astronomy refined it further. Both are dead practices. The Surya Siddhanta's constants are still running in production, computing real dates for real events, fifteen centuries after they were encoded.

## Sade Sati is division

Saturn: 29.5 years across 12 signs. 29.5 ÷ 12 ≈ 2.46 years per sign. Three signs around your birth Moon = 7.4 years, rounded to 7.5. "Sade sati" — seven and a half. The orbit was measured. The astrology was derived from it.

## The question the prime number raises

488,203 is prime. It's also the Bhagana for the Moon's apogee — an invisible point inferred from subtle variations in lunar speed. This single number, by being indivisible, forces the entire Mahayuga to be exactly 4,320,000 years. Not a cosmological preference. Not a philosophical choice. A mathematical constraint imposed by one awkward, stubborn prime.

Did the astronomers discover this number through patient observation of the Moon's speed variations across 3,232-day cycles? Did a mind in meditation perceive the Moon's elliptical orbit as a geometric truth before anyone tracked the data? Or did someone, working with eight orbital periods that needed a smaller LCM, add the ninth — the apogee — and watch the number lock to 4,320,000, the same number their cosmological tradition had always held?

The numbers don't answer. They just factorise cleanly.
