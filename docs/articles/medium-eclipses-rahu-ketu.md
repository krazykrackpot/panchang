# Rahu and Ketu Are Not Demons — They're the Geometry That Makes Eclipses Possible

*How ancient Indian astronomers turned orbital mechanics into mythology, and why eclipses only happen twice a year*

---

Every six months or so, the Sun or Moon disappears. Not metaphorically — the sky goes dark in the middle of the day, or the full Moon turns blood-red at night. For most of human history, this was terrifying.

The Hindu mythological explanation: a demon named **Rahu** swallows the Sun. When the gods catch him, Vishnu slices him in half — but it's too late, he's already consumed the nectar of immortality. So Rahu lives on as a severed head that periodically swallows the Sun, while his severed tail, **Ketu**, does the same to the Moon.

It's a great story. It's also, when you decode it, **a precise description of orbital geometry**.

## The Two Planes

Here's what's actually happening.

The Earth orbits the Sun. From our perspective on Earth, this makes the Sun appear to travel across the sky along a path called the **ecliptic** — a great circle tilted about 23.4 degrees from the equator.

The Moon orbits the Earth. But the Moon's orbital plane is NOT aligned with the ecliptic. It's tilted by about **5.14 degrees**.

This tilt is everything. If the Moon orbited in exactly the same plane as the Sun, we'd get a solar eclipse at every new Moon and a lunar eclipse at every full Moon — roughly every two weeks. The sky would go dark twice a month.

But because the two planes are tilted relative to each other, the Sun and Moon are usually at different "heights" when they line up. The Moon passes above or below the Sun at new Moon, and above or below Earth's shadow at full Moon. No eclipse.

**Except** at two specific points.

## The Nodes: Where the Planes Cross

Two tilted planes always intersect along a line. The Moon's orbital plane and the ecliptic intersect at exactly two points, called the **lunar nodes**.

- **Ascending node**: where the Moon crosses the ecliptic going north
- **Descending node**: where the Moon crosses going south

In Vedic astronomy, these are **Rahu** (ascending) and **Ketu** (descending).

An eclipse can ONLY happen when three conditions are met simultaneously:

1. It's either a new Moon (Sun-Moon conjunction) or full Moon (Sun-Moon opposition)
2. The Sun is near one of the two nodes
3. The Moon is near the same node (solar eclipse) or the opposite node (lunar eclipse)

That's it. That's the entire mechanism. The Sun must be close to Rahu or Ketu at the moment of a new or full Moon. If it's too far from a node, the Moon's 5.14-degree tilt means it passes above or below the alignment, and there's no eclipse.

## Why the "Demon Swallows the Sun" Story Is Geometrically Perfect

Think about what Rahu "does" in the mythology:

- Rahu is a **point** — a severed head, not a body. The ascending node is also a mathematical point, not a physical object.
- Rahu "swallows" the Sun — an eclipse occurs when the Sun reaches Rahu's position.
- Ketu (the tail) does the same to the Moon — a lunar eclipse occurs when the full Moon is near Ketu.
- Rahu is described as moving **backwards** through the zodiac — and indeed, the lunar nodes regress (move in the opposite direction to the planets) at about 19.36 degrees per year.

The mythology didn't merely observe that eclipses happen. It encoded the mechanism: **two points, moving backwards through the sky, that cause eclipses when the Sun or Moon reaches them**.

The Surya Siddhanta (5th century CE) formalises this by giving Rahu an orbital period of approximately 18.6 years — the time it takes the nodes to complete one full backward revolution. This is the **nodal precession period**, and NASA confirms it at **18.613 years**.

## Eclipse Seasons: Why They Come in Pairs

The Sun takes one year to travel around the ecliptic. The two nodes are roughly opposite each other (180 degrees apart). So the Sun passes near a node approximately every **6 months**.

Each time the Sun is near a node, there's a window of about 34-38 days (called an **eclipse season**) during which eclipses are possible. If a new Moon falls within this window, we get a solar eclipse. If a full Moon falls within it, we get a lunar eclipse. Usually, at least one of each happens per season.

This is why eclipses come in pairs (or triplets): a solar eclipse followed by a lunar eclipse about two weeks later, or vice versa. They're not independent events — they're both caused by the Sun being near the same node.

But here's the twist: because the nodes are **moving backwards**, the eclipse seasons shift earlier each year by about 19 days. A solar eclipse in June 2026 means the next eclipse season will centre around late May 2027, not June. Over 18.6 years, the eclipse seasons cycle through every month of the calendar.

## The Saros Cycle: Eclipses That Repeat

The most elegant pattern in eclipse astronomy is the **Saros cycle** — a period of **6,585.3 days** (approximately 18 years, 11 days, 8 hours) after which nearly identical eclipses recur.

Why does this work?

Three orbital periods need to align:

1. **Synodic month** (new Moon to new Moon): 29.53059 days
2. **Draconic month** (node to same node): 27.21222 days
3. **Anomalistic month** (perigee to perigee): 27.55455 days

After one Saros period:
- 223 synodic months = 6,585.32 days
- 242 draconic months = 6,585.36 days
- 239 anomalistic months = 6,585.54 days

These three numbers converge to nearly the same value. That means after one Saros, the Sun-Moon geometry is almost exactly the same: same phase (new or full), same distance from a node, same Moon-Earth distance. So you get a near-identical eclipse.

The 0.3-day remainder (about 8 hours) means each successive Saros eclipse shifts roughly 120 degrees west in longitude. After **three** Saros cycles (54 years, 34 days — called an **Exeligmos**), the eclipse returns to approximately the same longitude.

Ancient astronomers — Babylonian, Greek, and Indian — all discovered versions of this cycle independently, because the pattern is unmistakable once you track eclipses over decades.

## Eclipse Series: The Family Tree of Eclipses

Each Saros cycle defines a **series** — a family of related eclipses, each one Saros period apart. A typical Saros series lasts about 1,200-1,400 years and produces 70-85 eclipses.

Here's what a Saros series looks like over its lifetime:

1. **Birth**: The series begins with a small partial eclipse near one of the Earth's poles.
2. **Youth**: Over several centuries, each successive eclipse moves toward the equator and becomes larger.
3. **Maturity**: The middle eclipses are total or annular, crossing the equator.
4. **Old age**: Eclipses shift toward the other pole and shrink.
5. **Death**: The series ends with a small partial eclipse near the opposite pole from where it began.

Right now, there are about **40 active Saros series** producing solar eclipses simultaneously. Each produces one eclipse every 18 years, and they're all at different stages of their lifecycle.

The total solar eclipse that crossed North America in April 2024? That was eclipse #30 of Saros series 139, born in 1501 CE and destined to die in 2763 CE. The next eclipse in that same family will occur on 8 May 2042.

## Rahu-Ketu Transit: The Vedic Eclipse Indicator

In the Vedic system, Rahu and Ketu are treated as **planets** (grahas) despite being mathematical points. They're assigned signs, houses, nakshatras, and periods (dashas) in a birth chart — just like Saturn or Jupiter.

But their primary astronomical role is eclipse indication. Vedic panchangams (almanacs) track Rahu's transit through the zodiac, and the rule is simple:

**When Rahu or Ketu are within ~18 degrees of the Sun at the time of a new or full Moon, an eclipse is possible.**

This 18-degree window corresponds to the eclipse limit — the maximum angular distance between the Sun and a node for an eclipse to occur. For a total solar eclipse, the limit is tighter (~11.5 degrees). For a partial eclipse, it extends to ~18.5 degrees.

The Vedic system calls the 18-day window around an eclipse the **Grahan Kaal** (eclipse period), during which specific rituals and precautions are observed. The timing is derived directly from Rahu's computed position.

## The Mythology Decoded

Here's the complete mapping between the mythology and the geometry:

| Mythology | Geometry |
|-----------|----------|
| Rahu (severed demon head) | Ascending lunar node — a mathematical point, not a body |
| Ketu (severed demon tail) | Descending lunar node — 180 degrees opposite Rahu |
| "Rahu swallows the Sun" | Solar eclipse: Sun conjunct Rahu at new Moon |
| "Ketu darkens the Moon" | Lunar eclipse: Moon conjunct Ketu (opposite Rahu) at full Moon |
| "Rahu moves backward" | Nodal regression: nodes move retrograde at ~19.4 degrees/year |
| "Eclipse lasts until Sun escapes" | Eclipse duration: time for Sun-Moon alignment to pass the node |
| The nectar of immortality | The nodes never disappear — they're permanent features of the geometry |
| Vishnu slicing Rahu in half | One node becoming two: the ascending and descending points of intersection |

The mythology isn't a corruption of the science. It's a **mnemonic device** — a story designed to be memorable enough to survive oral transmission across centuries, encoding the essential truth that eclipses are caused by two invisible, backward-moving points in the sky.

The ancient astronomers knew exactly what Rahu and Ketu were. They chose to tell a story about it instead of writing an equation. Given that the story survived 2,000+ years while most equations didn't, it was arguably the better engineering decision.
